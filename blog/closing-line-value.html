CLOSING-LINE VALUE — THE ONLY METRIC THAT MATTERS.

Published 2026-05-05 · ~8 min read

If you bet sports — or follow anyone who does — there's one number you should care about more than any other. It's not win rate. It's not units profit. It's not return on investment. It's closing-line value, or CLV. Here's what it is, why it's the gold standard, and how to use it to evaluate any prediction source (including ours).

The problem with win rate

The most common thing people brag about: "I'm hitting 56% on my picks!" Sounds great. Probably means nothing.

Win rate is noisy. Over a small sample, even genuinely random picks can show win rates above or below their true value by a wide margin. A coin-flipper has a 50% true win rate, but in any given 30-pick stretch they could easily hit 60% — or 40%. The variance is huge.

The math:

  • 30 picks at a true 50% win rate → 95% confidence interval is 32–68%.
  • 100 picks at a true 50% win rate → 95% CI is 40–60%.
  • 1,000 picks at a true 50% win rate → 95% CI is 47–53%.

So a tipster showing you a 60% win rate over 30 picks could just be on a lucky streak with no actual edge. Even a 100-pick sample at 60% (CI 50–70%) leaves room for the underlying skill to be exactly 50% — pure noise.

You'd need thousands of picks for win rate alone to be statistically meaningful. Almost no public bettor or tipster has that record — and the ones who do are usually selectively reporting.

The problem with units profit

"Up +18.4 units last month!" Also potentially meaningless. Profit is path-dependent. A bettor who got lucky on three big-priced longshots can show a positive ROI for years before the variance catches up. A bettor with genuine edge who hit a cold streak can show negative ROI for a year.

Profit is the outcome you want, but it's not the metric you can measure skill with. Skill shows up over thousands of bets. Most public records aren't long enough.

What CLV actually is

CLV stands for closing-line value. It's the difference between the line you bet at and the line that closes (when the game starts).

Example: You bet the Lakers at -3 on Friday morning. By Sunday afternoon at tipoff, the line has moved to Lakers -4. You "got" -3, but the market closed at -4. You beat the closing line by 1 point.

That's positive CLV. Whether the Lakers actually win or lose by 5 doesn't matter for the CLV calculation — what matters is that you took a price the market eventually concluded was wrong (in your favor).

Why CLV is the gold standard

The closing line is, statistically, the most efficient price the market produces for any given game. By the time the game starts:

  • Every public bettor has placed their money.
  • Every sharp bettor has placed their money.
  • Every injury, weather change, and lineup announcement has been priced in.
  • Sportsbooks have rebalanced their books based on action.

The closing line is, in effect, the wisdom-of-crowds answer with everyone's information aggregated. Beating it consistently means you have access to (or have computed) information the market doesn't have yet.

This is why professional bettors and sportsbooks both treat CLV as the only meaningful metric. Sportsbooks track CLV per customer — bettors who consistently beat the close get their stake limited or get banned. Bettors who lag the close are kept around.

The key insight: CLV is the metric the books use to decide who's beating them — so it's the metric you should use to decide if you're beating them. Win rate doesn't survive variance. CLV does.

How to compute CLV

For a single bet, the simple formula:

  • For sides/totals: CLV (in points) = your line − closing line. If you bet -3 and the close was -4, your CLV is +1 point. If you bet +6.5 and the close was +5, your CLV is +1.5 points.
  • For moneylines: Convert both prices to implied probability. CLV = closing implied probability − your implied probability. If you got +200 (33.3% implied) and the close was +150 (40%), your CLV is −6.7%. (You took worse odds than the close — negative CLV.)

For a sample of bets, take the average. A bettor or model averaging +2% CLV over 200+ picks has demonstrated edge that's almost statistically certain to be real.

What "good" CLV looks like

Average CLVWhat it means
−2% or worseYou're on the wrong side of every closing move. Net loser long-term, even if profitable short-term.
−0.5% to +0.5%Roughly market-efficient. No edge. You'll lose to vig over time.
+1% to +3%Genuine edge. Most "sharp" bettors land here.
+3% to +5%Strong edge. Range where most successful syndicate bettors and AI models perform.
+5%+Exceptional. Either a small sample, or you're betting markets that are still very inefficient (low-volume sports, niche props).

Pick1's 30-day rolling CLV is +3.8% at -110 odds. That's in the strong-edge band — and the model has generated enough picks for that number to be statistically meaningful.

Why CLV beats every other metric

Three reasons.

  1. It's hard to fake. Win rate can be cherry-picked. CLV requires comparing your bet line to the closing line on every bet — a record only available to people who actually placed (or recorded) the bet at a specific time. Tipsters showing screenshots of cashed parlays can't fake CLV without making up data.
  2. It converges fast. Win rate needs thousands of picks to be statistically meaningful. CLV converges on edge within hundreds — often within months for an active bettor.
  3. It's the metric the other side uses. Sportsbooks track CLV. Professional syndicates track CLV. If you want to know if you're beating the people whose job is to beat you, the metric they use is the metric you should use.

How to use CLV when evaluating any prediction source

If you're trying to decide whether to follow a tipster, an AI model, or any other sports-prediction source, ask three questions:

  1. Do they track and publish CLV? If no, dismiss them. Anyone serious about edge tracks this. Tipsters who only show "5-1 last week!" don't because they can't.
  2. Is their average CLV positive over a meaningful sample? Look for at least 200 picks with a positive average. Below 200 picks, even a +2% CLV could be variance.
  3. Is the sample current? A 2018 record of +5% CLV doesn't tell you what they're doing in 2026. Markets evolve; models drift. The relevant CLV is the last 3–6 months.

This single filter eliminates 99% of paid sports-prediction services. Most don't survive it because most don't have the edge they claim.
